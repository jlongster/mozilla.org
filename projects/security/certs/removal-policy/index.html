When distributing binary and source code versions of Firefox, Thunderbird, and other Mozilla-related software products the Mozilla Foundation and its wholly-owned subsidiary the Mozilla Corporation include with such software a default set of X.509v3 certificates for various Certification Authorities (CAs). The certificates included by default have their "trust bits" set for various purposes, so that the software in question can use the CA certificates to verify certificates for SSL servers, S/MIME email users, and digitally-signed code objects without having to ask users for further permission or information.

This is the work-in-progress to create an official Mozilla Foundation policy for the deprecation and removal of CA certificates from software products that the Mozilla Foundation and Mozilla Corporation distribute

  1. We reserve the right to remove a particular CA certificate from our products, or to modify the "trust bits" for a particular CA certificate included in our products, at any time and for any reason. This includes (but is not limited to) cases where we believe that continuing to include a CA certificate (or setting its "trust bits" in a particular way) would cause undue risks to users' security, for example, with CAs that
    • knowingly issue certificates without the knowledge of the entities whose information is referenced in the certificates; or
    • knowingly issue certificates that appear to be intended for fraudulent use.
    • have not provided evidence to Mozilla of a recent audit satisfying sections 8, 9, and 10 of the Mozilla CA Policy.
    This also includes (but again is not limited to) cases where we believe that continuing to include a CA certificate (or setting its "trust bits" in a particular way) might cause technical problems with the operation of our software, for example, with CAs that issue certificates that have
    • ASN.1 DER encoding errors;
    • invalid public keys (e.g., DSA certificates with 2048-bit primes, or RSA certificates with public exponent equal to 1);
    • duplicate issuer names and serial numbers;
    • incorrect extensions (e.g., SSL certificates that exclude SSL usage, or authority key IDs that include both the key ID and the issuer's issuer name and serial number); or
    • cRLDistributionPoints or OCSP authorityInfoAccess extensions for which no operational CRL or OCSP service exists.
  2. We reserve the right to remove a particular CA certificate from our products when we believe that attacks on roots with older signature algorithms or smaller key lengths are imminent and/or readily repeatable. For example, CAs using
    • MD2, MD4, MD5, or SHA1 signature algorithms
    • Key lengths of 1024 bit or smaller
    In these situations we will follow this process:
    • Assess root certificates in Mozilla products for vulnerability to the specific attack.
    • Attempt to contact affected CAs and inform them of the attack and its projected consequences for their root certificates. Likely this will include information on our intention to remove affected root certificates from distribution, and the tentative schedule to do so. Communication may be done by posting to a Mozilla newsgroup and/or sending an email to the affected CAs. Note: If we do not receive a response from an affected CA within 48 hours of our communication, we may make the assumption of disinterest, and remove the affected root certificate(s) without further notification.
    • Remove all affected root certificates from distribution within a short time frame, and distribute a mandatory security update.
  3. We will determine which CA certificates may be removed from software products distributed through mozilla.org, based on the benefits and risks of such removal to typical users of those products.
  4. We will determine which "trust bits" may be unset for CA certificates distributed through mozilla.org, based on the benefits and risks of the "trust bit" settings to typical users of those products.
  5. We will make such decisions through a public process, based on objective and verifiable criteria as described below.
  6. We will not charge any fees to have a CA's certificate(s) removed from our software products.
  7. We will consider deprecating a CA certificate by unsetting one or more of the "trust bits" upon request for the following reasons
    • Expired or Expiring CA
    • Small modulus key length
    • Outdated signing key algorithm
    • Transition/Rollover to new root completed
    • Legacy, no longer in use
    • We reserve the right to use other considerations in the future.
  8. We will consider removing a CA certificate upon request for the following reasons
    • Security Compromise
    • Small modulus key length
    • Outdated signing key algorithm
    • Previously deprecated
    • We reserve the right to use other considerations in the future.
  9. We require that all CAs whose certificates are to be deprecated or removed from our software products:
    • Provide the reason for requesting deprecation or removal of the root certificate.
    • Publicly disclose the impact that deprecating or removing the root certificate may have on Mozilla users.
    • Prior to deprecating or removing the CA certificate, confirm that appropriate notification has been provided to users who may be negatively impacted.
  10. The burden is on the CA to prove that it has met the above requirements.
  11. To request that its certificate(s) be deprecated (one or more of the "trust bits" unset) or removed from the default set a CA should submit a formal request by submitting a bug report into the mozilla.org Bugzilla system, filed against the "CA Certificates" component of the "mozilla.org" product. The request should include the following:
    • The certificate data identifying the root certificate(s) to be deprecated or removed.
    • For each CA certificate requested to be removed, the reason that the certificate is to be removed.
    • For each CA certificate requested to be deprecated the reason that the certificate is to be deprecated, and a recommendation as to which "trust bits" should be unset.
    • For each CA certificate requested to be deprecated or removed, the impact that deprecation or removal may have on Mozilla users.
    • Information as to how the CA will ensure that deprecation or removal of the certificate will not cause users technical problems with the operation of our software.
    We will reject requests where the CA does not provide such information within a reasonable period of time after submitting its request.
  12. We will appoint a CA certificate "module owner" to evaluate CA requests on our behalf and make decisions regarding all matters relating to deprecating or removing CA certificates from our products. CAs or others objecting to a particular decision may appeal to mozilla.org staff, who will make a final decision.
  13. We reserve the right to change this policy in the future. We will do so only after consulting with the public Mozilla community, in order to ensure that all views are taken into account.

This policy applies only to software products distributed by the Mozilla Foundation and the Mozilla Corporation; other entities distributing such software are free to adopt their own policies. In particular, under the terms of the relevant Mozilla license(s) distributors of such software are permitted to add or delete CA certificates in the versions that they distribute, and are also permitted to modify the values of the "trust bits" on CA certificates in the default CA certificate set. As with other software modifications, by making such changes a distributor may affect its ability to use Mozilla trademarks in connection with its versions of the software; see the Mozilla trademark policy for more information.

Please contact the Mozilla Foundation at certificates@mozilla.org for more information about this policy and answers to related questions.
